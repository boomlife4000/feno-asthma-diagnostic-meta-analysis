How to read this tool

Select the age group and set the pre-test probability for the referral pathway. The tool returns two thresholds that divide FeNO into three zones:

  • Below the rule-out threshold (FeNO < rule-out): airway hyperresponsiveness is unlikely, and asthma can be excluded without a bronchial provocation test.
  • Above the rule-in threshold (FeNO ≥ rule-in): a positive provocation test is likely.
  • Between the two: this is the grey zone, where neither rule applies and a bronchial provocation test is most informative.

Selecting pre-test probability

Ideally, the pre-test probability for a referral pathway should be assessed against your own setting, to best inform use of this tool in your community. The clinically relevant estimates provided here are drawn from our tertiary centre, our primary-care referrals, and the pooled prevalence of asthma across the studies in our meta-analysis: primary care ~20%, specialist care ~40% in adults (~56% in children), and patient-reported physician-diagnosed asthma ~67%.1 Select the context closest to your patient, or set your own with the slider.

Selecting NPV and PPV thresholds

The suggested operating thresholds are rule-in at PPV ≥80% and rule-out at NPV ≥90% combined with sensitivity ≥80%.1 The sensitivity requirement matters because, in a low-prevalence setting, NPV can stay high while sensitivity is only modest, so a high-NPV rule-out can still miss a large share of asthmatics; requiring sensitivity ≥80% caps that miss at roughly one in five and, in adults at ~20% prevalence, lowers the rule-out from about 30 to < 20 ppb. The rule-out is held to a higher confidence than the rule-in because a missed diagnosis carries more risk than over-diagnosis: a patient with undiagnosed asthma remains at risk of exacerbations and untreated asthma complications, whereas a patient with a false-positive result could be exposed to unwarranted asthma therapy. This follows the logic of bronchial provocation testing itself, whose cut-points are chosen for high sensitivity and negative predictive value, so that a negative test reliably excludes current asthma.3 Raising the NPV or sensitivity requirement reduces missed asthma but narrows the rule-out zone; raising the PPV reduces over-diagnosis but raises the rule-in threshold.

Interpretation and usage

The three zones (rule-out, grey zone, and rule-in) quantify how the chosen thresholds would classify a patient. The grey zone widens as a higher confidence level is required, and at some prevalences a threshold is not achievable (for example, no rule-out at high prevalence, or no rule-in at low prevalence in children). Applied this way at a prevalence of 20% in our centre, the thresholds classified approximately 81% of primary-care referrals and were correct in approximately 90%, suggesting that around 80% of referrals for bronchial provocation testing could be averted.1

A multimodal approach

Biomarkers are best interpreted in clinical context rather than in isolation, analogous to obstructive sleep apnoea, where a high pre-test probability calculated by simple tools such as STOP-BANG determines the value of portable testing. Pre-test probability rises with supportive features; for example, Schneider et al. diagnosed asthma in primary care with a high degree of certainty in the presence of allergic rhinitis and wheezing at a FeNO of 33 ppb. A multivariable tool to refine pre-test probability remains a research priority.

Limitations

1 · ICS suppresses FeNO
Inhaled corticosteroids lower FeNO, so a low value in a treated patient can be a false negative. These thresholds were derived largely from ICS-naïve patients; in DIVE, a ≤30 ppb rule-out retained an NPV of 98% in ICS-naïve patients versus 88% on ICS. Where possible, measure FeNO before ICS or after at least two weeks of withdrawal, as exhaled nitric oxide may remain suppressed for around 18 days.1,2
2 · Rule-out misses (false negatives)
A rule-out misclassifies 1 − NPV of cases (about 7% at the adult rule-out of < 20 ppb and ~8% for children at < 15 ppb, and less at a higher NPV), compared with under 5% for a bronchial provocation test.1 A false negative carries greater consequence than a false positive, as a missed diagnosis leaves an at-risk patient untreated; persistent suspicion despite a low FeNO, particularly with features of type-2-low asthma, warrants further investigation.
3 · Rule-in misses (false positives)
A rule-in misclassifies 1 − PPV of cases (approximately 20% at the suggested PPV ≥80%, and fewer at a higher PPV). A raised FeNO is not specific to asthma; eosinophilic bronchitis, sinonasal and allergic disease, and indirect airway hyperresponsiveness may also raise it. Because a rule-in leads to a confirmatory provocation test, a false positive carries less consequence than a missed diagnosis.
4 · Children: not a standalone primary-care test
No FeNO threshold met rule-in likelihood-ratio criteria below ~40% pre-test probability in children; acceptable rule-in performance appears only in higher-prevalence (specialty) settings. The tool disables rule-in where it is not achievable.
5 · Blood eosinophils are not included
The blood eosinophil count performed weakly in adults (AUC ~0.65), added no value beyond FeNO, and no paediatric data were available; it is therefore not included here.
6 · Uncontrolled confounders
FeNO varies with age, height, ethnicity, atopy, smoking, measurement method, time of day, and day-to-day variation, none of which could be controlled in the meta-analysis; a single value should be interpreted accordingly.
7 · FeNO predicts airway hyperresponsiveness, not asthma
The reference standard throughout is a bronchial provocation test (PD20/PC20). FeNO reflects type-2 airway inflammation and predicts airway hyperresponsiveness; a diagnosis of asthma requires the full clinical context.
8 · Validation is single-centre and in adults
Prospective validation (DIVE) was conducted at a single centre in adults, using a protocolised referral pathway. Validation across multiple sites and in children is warranted, and generalisability to other settings may be limited.

Common questions

Can I still rule out if my patient is on ICS?
Use caution. ICS can suppress FeNO, so a low value may not exclude asthma. The rule-out was validated in steroid-naïve work-up. Consider testing before/without ICS, or proceed to bronchial provocation testing if suspicion persists.
Why is FeNO high when the provocation test is negative?
This is expected in approximately 20% (false positives): eosinophilic bronchitis, sinonasal and allergic disease, and indirect airway hyperresponsiveness can raise FeNO when the bronchial provocation test is negative.
FeNO is low but I still suspect asthma
About 7% of ruled-out adults (and ~8% of children) are false negatives, compared with under 5% for a bronchial provocation test. If clinical suspicion remains high, particularly with features of type-2-low asthma, further investigation is warranted.
Does device or time of day matter?
Yes. Use a standardised device; values vary with device, time of day, atopy, smoking, height, ethnicity, and from day to day, so a single reading should be interpreted with these in mind.

Evidence at a glance

Meta-analysis: 30 studies, >8,000 patients with suspected asthma and normal spirometry. Adult FeNO AUC 0.80 (k=22); paediatric FeNO AUC 0.75 (k=9).

Prospective validation (DIVE, adults, single-centre): n=123, asthma prevalence 18%, FeNO AUC 0.77 (95% CI 0.65–0.89), with stronger discrimination in steroid-naïve patients.
